Research

Mining Online Learning Activities
Last Updated: May 18th, 2018 9pm EST

Article: TBP

Context

Over the course of the next year (May 2018 - April 2019), I will be working under the supervision of Professor Andrew Petersen to identify and formulate learning goals to eliminate student struggles in introductory computer science courses (CS1). At the University of Toronto, many professors use video lessons, reading guides, MCQ's and small coding exercises to aid in teaching CS1 courses, through an online portal known as PCRS. These modules are typically due before lectures, to expose students to new ideas and to allow more time to address misunderstandings in the content.

In addition to PCRS, computer science students at the University of Toronto have access to the discussion board, an online forum hosted by the computer science department. Each course has a designated page, each containing various categories (i.e. Assignment 1, Labs, Midterm, etc.) where students can open threads to gain feedback from their instructors, TAs and fellow students. These forums become particularly busy close to deadlines or test/exams and are kept relatively clean by instructors removing inappropriate student content. They provide a great way for students to not only address their misconceptions, whether publicly or anonymously, but allow students to engage in meaningful computer science related discussion.

Patterns

Traditional pedagogies have been shown to be ineffective for teaching CS1 courses. With the unique and advantageous resources available to computer science students at the University of Toronto, in addition to traditional ones such as office hours, failure and drop rates continue to be a tremendous problem. Moreover, it is evident that computer science students are not learning as much as instructors expect with the inability to use abstraction to tackle complex problems.

Integrating data from both PCRS problem attempts and discussion board activities, we will be applying rigorous machine learning, combined with semantic and sentimental analysis, to explore how students engage with computer science material. Using this data set, we desire to formulate concrete learning goals to aid in the teaching of future CS1 courses and improve student retention of key material. Our implementation will be done primarily in Python.
